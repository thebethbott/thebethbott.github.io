U.S. Supreme Court

"The Court is the highest tribunal in the Nation for all cases and controversies arising under the Constitution or the laws of the United States. As the final arbiter of the law, the Court is charged with ensuring the American people the promise of equal justice under law and, thereby, also functions as guardian and interpreter of the Constitution." — SupremeCourt.gov

Introduction

The Supreme Court's primary role is to maintain the status quo by enforcing the laws of the legislative branch. Its eight justices have the discretion to hear cases on a variety of issues. Some of the cases they choose to hear, seek to push the interpretation of laws beyond maintaining the status quo and have a lasting impact on the legacy of the Court and the course our society. Cases discussing privacy, for example, have altered the course of history propelling both the LGBTQIA+ and Women's movements forward. If used too liberally, this power gives these eight unelected officials broad unchecked power.


Total Cases Discussed by Issue

To understand the degree of cumulative Judicial power since its incarnation, below we compare cases categorized by overarching distinctive issue areas.

The primary issue areas at the forefront of the Court's cumulative caseload include:

  1. Criminal procedure- cruel and unusual punishment, right to council, and illegal search and seizure

  2. Economic activity- wage arbitration, property claims, and corruption

  3. Civil rights- voting rights, desegregation, and discrimination


Analysis

Rationally to maintain the status quo, the Court's role to protect property and safety are at the fundamental core of our rule of law society— logically these have been the issues most frequently reviewed in Supreme Court cases: 1) Criminal Procedure followed by 2) Economic Activity. The court has adjudicated far fewer cases that fundamentally change our rights and protections related to, for example, free speech and organized labor.

However, one issue area that has substantially impacted our social order is Civil Rights. Given the high percentage of cases reviewed in the Civil Rights issue area, as the third most deliberated issue area in the Court to-date, the protection of Civil Rights can be attributed as a primary function of the Court. These data reflect a common thought that the Supreme Court is in fact an "Activist Court" that creates its own laws and furthers social movement, progress, or change. From affirmative action and integration to voting rights and jurisdiction limitations over indigenous peoples the Court has played a powerful role in our society, despite being removed from the democratic election system required by our other branches of government.

While the Court remains true to its function as the constitution's guardian, the Court has exercised its power as interpreter to adjudicate beyond the scope of existing agreed upon laws. As parties continue to change and the country becomes increasing divided, this unchecked power has an unstable stronghold on our democracy.
